Parachute Drop Lab

Introduction

What slows a parachute when it falls? A parachute is slowed by air resistance. Air is a collection of gas molecules that have to be pushed out of the way for any object to move through it. These molecules can exert force (pressure) on objects that try to push them out of the way. This is an example of Newton's 3rd Law (for every action there is an equal and opposite reaction).

Parachutes can also be used to study energy changes. As a future aerospace engineer, youâ€™re going to use the process of creating and deploying parachutes to study the Law of Conservation of Energy. Procedure

  1. Choose a set height (at least 2 meters; higher is better) from which to drop the pencil and parachute. One student should drop the object while another times the falls and records the data on the DATA SHEET.
  2. From the plastic bags cut out 2 different sizes of squares. Measure the size and record in the data chart. (cut one that is a 10cm x 10cm square and another that is 20cm x 20cm)
  3. Cut the string into FOUR equal lengths of about 25 cm each.
  4. Use a small piece of tape to secure the end of one string to one corner of your parachute.
  5. Tape the second string to the second corner, the third string to the third corner, and the fourth string to the fourth corner of your parachute.
  6. Collect the strings (making sure they are not tangled) and tape all four free ends to one end of the pencil.
  7. Find the mass of your parachute (in kg)and record this in Parachute 1 Data Chart Trial #1 and Trial #2
    Why the same mass?
  8. Drop the parachute from your selected height and time the fall. Before you release the parachute, be sure it is open and free from tangles.
  9. Enter the time of the fall and the area of the parachute in the Parachute 1 Data Chart Trial #1
  10. Repeat one more time, and enter that time for Trial #2 on the data sheet.
  11. Attach strings to your second parachute as you did for your first one.
  12. Find the mass of your parachute (in kg)and record this in Parachute 2 Data Chart Trial #1 and Trial #2
  13. Drop the parachute from your selected height and time the fall. Before you release the parachute, be sure it is open and free from tangles.
  14. Enter the time of the fall and the area of this 2nd parachute in the Parachute 2 Data Chart Trial #1
  15. Repeat the procedure one more time, and enter those times for Trial #2.
  16. Calculate GPE, Velocity, KE, and Total Energy (see equations in the Data Analysis section)

Data Analysis

  • Find average velocity by using the equation: \[v_{ave}=\frac{height}{time}\]
  • Find the final velocity by using the equation: \[v_{final}=v_{ave}*2\]
  • Find G.P.E. by using the equation:
    \[G.P.E=m*g*h\]
    Record this G.P.E. box for each trial
  • Find the K.E. by using the equation:
    \[K.E.=\frac{1}{2}*m*v^2\]
    Record this in the K.E. box for each trial
  • Find the difference in energy by using the equation:
    \[\Delta E = K.E. - G.P.E.\]
