The Speed of Sound

Have you ever noticed that you always see a firework go off before you actually hear the sound? Why is that? We will be investigating this phenonmenon by measuring the speed of sound. Ideally, in order to get a measurement we would need to make a sound with a visual cue and then time how long it takes for the sound to reach our ears. There are many sources of error in this method however, and it also requires far more space than we have. Instead we will be looking at sound waves with technology!

Procedure

  1. Record the temperature of the air in the room
  2. Connect the Microphone to the LabQuest
  3. From the 'File' menu, select 'New'
    • Remember to always discard the old data
  4. Zero your Microphone
  5. Click the box that says 'Mode'
  6. Enable triggering by clicking on the triangle next to 'Triggering'
    1. Check the box for 'Enable Triggering'
    2. Make sure the first drop down is CH1: Microphone
    3. Be sure to select the 'Increasing' radio button
    4. change 'across' to 0.1
    5. collect 0 points before trigger
    6. Press okay
  7. Hold the Microphone right next to the open end of the tube.
  8. Press play on the LabQuest
  9. Check to make sure the LabQuest is awaiting data
  10. Let the arms of the binderclip click together 2-3 inches from the opening of the tube
    • If your noise is too loud it will cause the graph to be very difficult to read
    >
  11. If successful you will get a graph that is similar to this.
  12. Find the time at the first peak on your graph. This is the initial click
  13. Find the time of the second peak on your graph. This is the echo from the tube
  14. Find the difference between the two times. This is how long the sound wave was traveling
  15. Record 5 trials
  16. Find the length of your tube

Data Analysis

  1. Determine your average time interval by averaging your time intervals
  2. Double the length of your tube. (Why do we need to do this?)
  3. Determine the speed of the wave using your doubled length and average time interval
    • Remember the equation for velocity:

    \[v={s \over t}\]

    • v - velocity
    • s - displacement
    • t - time
