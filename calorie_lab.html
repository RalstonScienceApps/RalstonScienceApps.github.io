Calorimetry of Fuels

The Energy-Work Connection

Introduction

Energy is crucial to the survival of every living thing. As humans, our bodies need to remain within a relatively narrow temperature range. We “burn food” (internally) to stay warm. Additionally, because all systems tend to move toward disorder without an input of energy, we would die without food. We need bodily fuel so that we can function, and we burn it when we walk, talk, sleep, exercise, and study. You may have heard of “burning calories” in reference to exercise. Please note that calories are never burned. A calorie is simply a measure of energy, and that energy is actually released during a combustion reaction. During exercise, the body burns food to release energy, and that energy can be measured in different units, such as calories. The evening before a marathon, runners are advised to eat a huge plate of pasta. Why? Because pasta is a carbohydrate, is a terrific source of energy, or fuel for the body. Different foods contain varying amounts of energy, or calories. In other words, calories are a way to measure the energy you get from the food you eat. Food contains Potential Energy stored in its chemical bonds. When we consume and digest the food we break the bonds and convert it into Kinetic Energy that we can use for motion.

In this activity, you will demonstrate how calories are measured. First, you need to build an inexpensive calorimeter. A calorimeter is a device that measures the transfer of heat energy during a chemical or physical change. One type of calorimeter contains a combustion chamber surrounded by water. When matter is placed in the chamber and physically or chemically changed (usually by burning), the temperature change of the surrounding water is measured and used to determine the energy (calorie) content of the sample. This lab indirectly measures food Calories (kilocalories) using a homemade calorimeter.

Materials

  • Ring Stand
  • 3 Large Paper Clips
  • Matches
  • Water
  • 100 mL Graduated Cylinder
  • cork
  • 12-oz Soda Can
  • Celcius Thermometer
  • Forceps or Tongs
  • coffee filter
  • Cheesy Puffs
  • Shredded Wheat or Marshmallow

Procedure

  1. Obtain a metal can. If necessary, wipe any carbon deposits off the bottom of the can using a paper towel.
  2. Use scissors to poke four small holes towards the top of the aluminum can.
  3. Place two straightened large paper clips into opposing holes.
  4. Hang the can in the wire ring on the ring stand and adjust the height accordingly.
  5. Add 200ml of water to the soda can. This apparatus is your calorimeter. Place the thermometer into the mouth of the can and record the temperature below. Remember to suspend the thermometer in the water when taking a temperature reading, DO NOT let it touch the bottom.
  6. Straighten half of the third paper clip and leave one the other end bent. Push the straight end into the cork and bend the other end to hold the cheesy puff.
  7. Grab a coffee filter and find its mass.
  8. Choose a cheesy puff and pierce it with the paper clip device, measure the initial mass and record in the data table.
  9. Place the cheesy puff stand directly under the calorimeter. Use a match to set the cheesy puff on fire. This may take several tries.
  10. Move the can over the burning cheesy puff and position it so that the top of the flame just touches the bottom of the can. Allow the cheesy puff to burn completely. If the flame goes out and the cheesy puff is not completely burned, relight. Remember to move the can away as you relight.
  11. As soon as the cheesy puff stops burning, IMMEDIATELY take a final water temperature reading and record it in the data table.
  12. As soon as the cheesy puff has cooled, use forceps to CAREFULLY transfer the remaining material to a PREWEIGHED piece of weighing paper. Take the cheesy puffs final mass and record in the data table.
  13. Repeat the procedure using a new cheesy puff.
  14. Repeat the procedure twice more using one different food material.
